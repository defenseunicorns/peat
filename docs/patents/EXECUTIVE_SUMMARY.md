## TL;DR - What We're Proposing

**File 2 provisional patents this week ($260) + publish defensive Patent Pledge, then open-source everything.**

This gives us defensive protection while addressing NATO/academic concerns about proprietary technology.

---

## The Opportunity

Peat Protocol has **two core innovations** that are patentable:

1. **Hierarchical Capability Composition**: How autonomous systems compose capabilities in hierarchies (squads, platoons) with O(n log n) efficiency using CRDTs
2. **Graduated Human Authority Control**: Five-level human oversight system (FULL_AUTO → MANUAL) that works in distributed, partition-prone networks

These innovations solve real problems for DoD autonomous systems and have commercial applications in IoT, robotics, and cloud orchestration.

---

## The Challenge

**Problem**: Patents might deter NATO allies or academic contributors who worry about proprietary lock-in.

**Solution**: File patents + publish Patent Pledge that explicitly protects:
- NATO member nations and defense organizations
- Academic and research institutions
- Open-source contributors
- Non-commercial use

**Precedent**: Google, Red Hat, Tesla all use this approach - patents for defense, pledge for openness.

---

## The Strategy

### Week 1: File Provisionals
- Cost: $260 (DIY) or $4K-$6K (with attorney)
- Establishes priority date (critical for first-to-file)
- Protects against competitors patenting same ideas

### Week 1-2: Publish Patent Pledge
- Legally binding promise not to assert patents against NATO/academia/OSS
- Addresses concerns about proprietary technology
- Strong signal of commitment to open collaboration

### Week 2: Open-Source Everything
- Now protected by patent priority + pledge
- Can publish code, papers, presentations freely
- Optionally use "Patent Pending" in materials

### Month 12 (Nov 2025): Decision Point
**Option A - Maximum Openness**: Abandon provisionals ($0 additional cost)
- Creates defensive prior art (blocks competitors)
- Pure GOTS/NATO standardization play
- No licensing revenue potential

**Option B - Defensive Protection**: Convert to utility patents ($20K-$40K)
- Can enforce against patent trolls/competitors
- Maintain Patent Pledge for NATO/academia
- Licensing revenue potential for commercial use

## Risks

### ⚠️ Perception Risk
**Risk**: Open-source community sees patents as "rug-pull"
**Mitigation**: Publish Patent Pledge immediately after filing, explicitly protect OSS contributors

### ⚠️ Cost Risk
**Risk**: Utility patents cost $40K-$80K for both
**Mitigation**: Only convert if strong evidence of value; can abandon at Month 12

### ⚠️ Enforcement Risk
**Risk**: Patents only valuable if we can/will enforce
**Mitigation**: Focus on defensive use; pledge not to sue except offensive assertion

### ⚠️ Prior Art Risk
**Risk**: Existing CRDT/distributed systems literature could invalidate claims
**Mitigation**: Claims focus on CAP-specific innovations (hierarchical composition, graduated authority); general CRDT infrastructure NOT patented

## Alternatives Considered

### Alternative 1: Don't File Patents
**Pros**: Zero cost, pure open-source narrative
**Cons**: No defensive protection, competitors can patent same ideas, no licensing revenue
**Verdict**: ❌ Too risky - leaves us vulnerable

### Alternative 2: File Patents, No Pledge
**Pros**: Maximum flexibility, intimidate competitors
**Cons**: Deters NATO allies and academics, contradicts GOTS narrative
**Verdict**: ❌ Defeats strategic goals

### Alternative 3: Keep Proprietary
**Pros**: Maximum IP control, clear business model
**Cons**: No NATO standardization, no academic collaboration, limits adoption
**Verdict**: ❌ Wrong for defense tech

### Alternative 4: File + Pledge (Recommended)
**Pros**: Defensive protection + NATO/academic friendly + open-source
**Cons**: Modest cost ($260-$40K), requires ongoing management
**Verdict**: ✅ Best balance

## FAQs

**Q: Why not wait until we have customers?**
**A**: Because we're about to open-source. Once public, we have 12-month grace period (US only) to file. Filing first gives us maximum flexibility.

**Q: What if we're acquired?**
**A**: Patents are company assets. Patent Pledge binds successors. Acquirer can't revoke pledge.

**Q: Can we still open-source with patents?**
**A**: Yes! Apache-2.0 license + Patent Pledge work together. Many successful OSS projects do this (Kubernetes, TensorFlow, etc.)

**Q: What if NATO allies object?**
**A**: Patent Pledge explicitly protects them. We can also join Open Invention Network for additional credibility.

**Q: How do we decide at Month 12?**
**A**: Based on evidence: competitor patents, customer demand, licensing interest, investor requirements. See PATENT_STRATEGY.md for decision framework.

**Q: Can we retract patents later?**
**A**: Yes! Can dedicate to public domain, donate to defensive pool (OIN), or simply abandon. Pledge is already published, so we're committed to defensive use regardless.
